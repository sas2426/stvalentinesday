The Moon goes through a cycle of eight distinct phases as it orbits Earth every 29.5 days. These phases aren't caused by Earth's shadow, but by the changing angle between the Sun, the Moon, and your eyes on Earth.

The 8 Phases in Order
The lunar cycle is divided into four primary phases (moments in time) and four intermediate phases (spans of time): 
New Moon: The Moon is between Earth and the Sun; its illuminated side faces away from us, making it invisible.
Waxing Crescent: A thin silver sliver appearing on the right side (in the Northern Hemisphere) as the Moon begins to "grow".
First Quarter: Half of the Moon's disk is illuminated. It is called a "quarter" because the Moon has completed 25% of its orbit.
Waxing Gibbous: More than half is illuminated and continues to grow towards full.
Full Moon: The Moon is on the opposite side of Earth from the Sun, revealing its entire day side.
Waning Gibbous: The Moon begins to "shrink," with more than half still lit, but losing light on the right side.
